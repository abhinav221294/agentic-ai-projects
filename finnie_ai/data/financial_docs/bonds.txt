Title: Bonds (Fixed Income Investment) Guide
Source: Finnie AI Knowledge Base

What are Bonds?

Bonds are fixed-income investment instruments where an investor lends money to a government, company, or organization in exchange for regular interest payments and the return of the principal amount at maturity.

How Bonds Work
An issuer (government or company) raises money by issuing bonds.
Investors buy these bonds and lend money to the issuer.
The issuer pays periodic interest, known as a coupon.
At the end of the bond’s term (maturity), the principal amount is repaid.
Bond prices may fluctuate in the market before maturity.
Types of Bonds
Government Bonds

Issued by the government.
Low risk and considered safe investments.

Corporate Bonds

Issued by companies.
Offer higher returns but come with higher risk than government bonds.

Municipal Bonds

Issued by local authorities or municipalities.
Used to fund public projects like infrastructure.

Zero-Coupon Bonds

Do not pay regular interest.
Issued at a discount and redeemed at face value.

Benefits of Bonds

Stable Income: Provide regular interest payments.
Lower Risk: Generally less risky than stocks.
Capital Preservation: Return of principal at maturity (subject to issuer reliability).
Diversification: Helps balance a portfolio with lower-risk assets.

What is Yield?

Yield refers to the return an investor earns from a bond, including interest payments and any capital gain or loss if the bond is bought or sold at a different price.

Risks Involved

Interest Rate Risk: Bond prices fall when interest rates rise.
Credit Risk: Issuer may fail to pay interest or principal.
Inflation Risk: Returns may not keep up with rising prices.
Liquidity Risk: Some bonds may be difficult to sell quickly.

Example

If an investor buys a bond worth Rs. 1000 with a 5% annual interest rate, they receive Rs. 50 each year as interest.
At maturity, the investor gets back the original Rs. 1000.

Final Thought

Bonds are a reliable investment option for generating steady income with relatively lower risk. They are suitable for conservative investors and for balancing a diversified portfolio.
